When the Metric Is the Problem

Amazon Alexa · Consumer Hardware · 2022

Inherited metrics are like inherited debt. They keep you paying interest on someone else's problem.
All case studies

The Setup

I inherited a metric that had been tracking product success for three years. It was a number that mattered to leadership, that looked good in dashboards, and that everyone had optimized around. But it was measuring the wrong thing.

What Was Hidden

The metric showed we were succeeding. But when I spent time with users, something was off. They weren't engaged the way the numbers suggested. The metric was capturing a slice of behavior, but missing the context of why that behavior mattered. Deeper analysis revealed: we had optimized for frequency, not satisfaction. Users were interacting with the product, but not in ways that created value for them. We had built a metric that rewarded our efforts, not their needs.

The Reframe

We didn't ask "How do we improve the metric?" We asked "What should we actually be measuring?" This led us to redesign success entirely:

  • Shift from frequency to intent — did users come because they wanted something specific?
  • Track emotional resolution, not just interaction
  • Measure retention through value, not habit

The Cost

In the short term, our numbers got worse. On the inherited metric, we regressed. But in real terms — in user satisfaction, in retention, in the kind of engagement that builds sustainable products — we improved dramatically.

The Insight

Product leaders inherit metrics like they inherit codebases. You start by assuming the foundation is right. But sometimes the most important work is questioning the foundation itself — and having the conviction to change it, even when the numbers initially get worse.
