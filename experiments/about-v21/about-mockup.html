04 · About

I study people before I build for them.

I work in product strategy, but sociology is still the lens through which I read every system I touch. Most hard product decisions are behavioral questions in disguise: what people are protecting, what they are afraid of, and what has to feel true before they act.

My story

I did not set out to become a sociologist. I arrived there by recognizing myself in it.

Point of view

Most product strategy stops at user psychology and UX.

I add sociology: norms, identity, status, trust.

Real social systems also run on power, structure, feedback, conflict, and value extraction.

If you ignore those, your “social” strategy is just surface deep.

I came to UCLA on the path that looked safest on paper. Biochemistry was legible. Respectable. Easy to explain. Then I took a sociology course and recognized something more native to me than certainty. It gave language to something I had already been doing instinctively: noticing the invisible rules underneath people, institutions, and environments.

Growing up between India and the United States made those rules impossible not to see. The needs were constant. The scripts changed. Belonging, safety, status, ambition, care. Same human forces. Different expressions. That lens stayed with me. It became useful in product because most product problems are human problems wearing technical clothes.

At Amazon, working on Alexa sharpened that instinct. Voice is a brutal teacher. When there is no screen to hide behind, you learn very quickly whether a product actually understands the person using it. The work made me more exacting about trust, friction, quality, and what a decision feels like inside someone’s day.

Selected range

Three chapters of the same curiosity.

Amazon

Product strategy in moments where trust breaks fast.

At Alexa, I worked across roadmap, monetization, and operating rhythm in one of the most behavior-sensitive product surfaces in consumer tech.

No Desk Project

Community building where structure no longer came built in.

I built for a generation of location-independent professionals trying to reconstruct belonging, momentum, and accountability outside traditional systems.

Books & Beds

The same thinking, translated into physical space.

I am increasingly drawn to the environments that shape how a person feels: what helps them arrive, exhale, and feel more fully themselves inside a place.

What I believe

Beliefs, not bullet points.

Rigor before reassurance

I would rather understand the real problem than produce a quick answer that flatters the room.

Behavior is data

I pay attention to what people do under pressure, what teams quietly avoid, and where friction persists after consensus.

Build for the specific human

Real people arrive with context, contradiction, mood, history, fatigue, and desire. The work gets better when we remember that.

Now

I am interested in work that changes how people feel inside a system.

Sometimes that system is a product. Sometimes it is a team. Sometimes it is a room, a rhythm, or a life someone is trying to build with more intention. Across all of it, I keep returning to the same work: seeing clearly, naming honestly, and designing with enough care that people can feel the difference.
