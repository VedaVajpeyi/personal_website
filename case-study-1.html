Crisis GTM: 50M Users in 13 Days

Aarogya Setu · Government of India · March 2020

Authority creates velocity — but legitimacy sustains adoption. Both must be built in parallel from day one.
All case studies

The Crisis

In March 2020, the Government of India launched Aarogya Setu, a contact tracing app, to help manage the COVID-19 pandemic. Within 13 days, it had been downloaded 50 million times — one of the fastest adoption curves for any government app globally. But adoption wasn't the problem. Trust was.

The Real Issue

The government's success in driving installs created an immediate credibility crisis. Users downloaded the app, but they didn't understand what it did or why they should use it. Worse, they didn't believe their data would be safe. Rumors spread. Opposition grew. The same velocity that created the download spike was feeding skepticism about the app's true purpose. This is where I was brought in: not to drive more downloads, but to reverse the erosion of trust.

What We Did

GTM strategy at this scale isn't about marketing — it's about structural clarity. We designed a communication architecture that:

  • Made the app's actual function unmistakable (contact tracing, not surveillance)
  • Showed users exactly what data was collected and what wasn't
  • Created multiple touchpoints for education — not just marketing, but genuine understanding
  • Addressed the unspoken fear: "Is the government watching me?"

The Outcome

What started as a crisis in credibility became a case study in how to communicate complex technology to a skeptical public. Active usage increased. Retention improved. More importantly, the narrative shifted — from "Why should I trust this?" to "Here's how this protects us."

The Lesson

Growth without clarity isn't growth — it's just noise. The difference between 50M users and 50M engaged users isn't more marketing. It's structural honesty about what you're asking people to do, and why.
